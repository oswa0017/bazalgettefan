Making Life in the City a Little Less Sh*tty

Stomach-Churning Reality


Back in the 1800s, London's population - particularly her poorest and most vulnerable - were ravaged by all sorts of infectious diseases - three chief among them being typhoid, typhus, and the ever-nasty cholera.


Cholera in particular was my area of interest. Countless cures were proferred for it, ranging from some truly ludicrous tinctures more likely to poison you, to miraculous remedies like "sucking on an ice cube." The layperson often looks back on these historical remedies as somehow stupid - and yet, even without our 21st century level of understanding, a lot of these cures are tackling it from a reasonable angle. Cholera in particular renders its victims feverish and dangerously dehyrated. A prescription of ice might help them feel better by tempering the fever, and more crucially, the eventual consumption of the ice would replenish water to a needy body.


It is a disease primarily transmitted by fecal contamination - a prospect far too disgusting for most of even the most open-minded scientists of the time. Whether they didn't believe it, or simply didn't want to, the prevailing theory was that you could catch cholera by breathing in the bad air - and THAT was why all those poor people in those smelly areas got sick. Not because of contaminated food and water. The Victorians were extremely reluctant to accept any theory of bacterial transmission that was tantamount to admitting they were eating... well, something that came out the other end, to put it delicately.
